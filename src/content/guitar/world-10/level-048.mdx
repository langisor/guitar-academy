# Level 48: Modal Interchange

## Lesson

Modal interchange (also called borrowed chords) is an advanced harmonic technique that involves borrowing chords from parallel modes to add color and emotion to your progressions. When you play in a major key, you can "borrow" chords from the parallel minor key—such as using a bIII, bVI, or bVII chord that normally wouldn't fit the key signature. For example, in the key of C major, you could borrow chords from C minor, like using an Eb chord (bIII) or Ab chord (bVI). This technique creates that sophisticated "jazzy" or "movie score" sound. The bVII chord (using Db in the key of C) is particularly powerful and appears in many popular songs. These borrowed chords add unexpected color while still feeling connected to the main key.

## Exercise

Apply modal interchange in your playing with this guided practice:
1. Start in the key of C major with a simple C to F to G progression
2. Now replace the F chord with an Fm chord—feel the emotional shift
3. The Fm is borrowed from C minor (bVI chord)—this is modal interchange
4. Try replacing G with G7, then experiment with Gb7 (the bVII of C major)
5. Practice the progression: C to Db to G7—feel the dramatic tension the Db creates
6. Try the bVII chord: instead of G to C, try F to C where F is the bVII of G
7. Practice: C to Am to F to G becomes C to Am to Fm to G (borrowing Fm from C minor)
8. The bIII chord: try adding an Eb chord in your C major progressions
9. Practice the "Andalusian" progression: Am to G to F to E
10. Create your own progression using one borrowed chord and notice how it transforms the mood
